Deep Dive

What Each Layer Means for Your Airport

From laying the energy foundation to enabling commercial e-plane operations — here's what airports need at each stage.

1
Landside

Energy Foundation

Before any vehicle or aircraft can charge, an airport needs a smart, resilient energy infrastructure. Solar generation, battery storage, and intelligent energy management systems are the bedrock of the entire pyramid — without this layer, all layers above become prohibitively expensive or unreliable.

Why start here: An airport's existing grid connection typically cannot handle the sudden surge in electrical load that EV charging brings. A smart energy foundation distributes load intelligently, reduces peak demand charges, and actively relieves grid congestion — rather than making it worse. Solar generation and battery storage buffer peaks before they hit the grid, and can generate revenue by providing grid balancing services.
Solar panels & carports Battery storage (BESS) Energy Management System Smart metering Green building standards
NRG2fly in practice
Teuge Airport — Solar panels on the main hangar roof directly supply the apron's charging network

At Teuge Airport, NRG2fly integrated solar generation into the airport's infrastructure from the start. The roof of the main hangar carries panels that feed directly into the charging network — keeping energy costs manageable from the very first charger and making the location energy-self-sufficient for all EV loads.

2
Landside

Landside Transport Charging

Electric ground transport is the most accessible and commercially compelling entry point into operational electrification. Airport buses, cargo trucks, shuttle vehicles, employee parking, and visitor parking can all switch to electric today using proven commercial technology — and many benefit from public subsidy programmes.

Why second: Familiar technology, straightforward procurement, eligible for public EV subsidies, and no aviation regulation complexity. Smart scheduling means charging happens off-peak, actively reducing grid congestion rather than adding to it. Most importantly, it builds electric vehicle operational knowledge across your entire airport staff before you move airside.
AC slow & DC fast chargers Fleet charging depot CCS2 / Type 2 Smart load management Public EV parking Grid congestion management
NRG2fly in practice
Den Helder Airport — Landside EV charging installed by NRG2fly

At Den Helder Airport, NRG2fly installed the first landside EV charging infrastructure — serving airport staff vehicles and visitors. This was the starting point for a broader electrification programme at the airport, building operational confidence and energy data before moving airside.

3
Airside

Ground Service Equipment Charging

Aircraft ground operations are rapidly electrifying. Baggage tractors, pushback tugs, belt loaders, catering trucks, and mobile ground power units are all available in electric variants today — and they spend most of their time between aircraft gates where opportunity charging is straightforward to implement.

Why third: GSE electrification drastically reduces apron emissions, lowers fuel costs, and introduces your ramp crew to airside electrical safety practices. Intelligent charge scheduling across the apron fleet keeps peak demand manageable — a critical step toward not overloading the grid as you scale up.
Gate opportunity charging GSE depot chargers High-capacity DC charging Smart charge scheduling Airside electrical safety

4
Airside

Small E-plane, eVtol & Drone Charging

Regional electric aircraft (up to ~19 seats), air taxis, and commercial drones are entering regular commercial service now. These aircraft need fast, reliable DC charging at gates and pads during short turnarounds — typically 20–45 minutes — with aviation-grade safety hardware and certified installation.

Why fourth: This layer requires aviation-certified charging hardware, airside safety integration, and coordination with air traffic management. The energy management built in Layers 1–3 ensures that aircraft charging happens within the grid's limits — no congestion spikes, no costly grid reinforcement surprises.
CPC-compliant chargers (22–150 kW) Vertiport infrastructure eVtol charging pads Drone docking & charging ATC integration

5
Airside

Large Commercial E-plane Charging

The apex of the pyramid. As commercial electric aircraft with 50+ seats enter service — expected from the late 2020s — airports with a complete electrification stack will be ready to serve them. High-power gate-integrated charging using the MCS (Megawatt Charging System) standard, from 1 MW up to 3+ MW per gate, is the ultimate destination. Only airports that have built every layer below — and managed grid congestion at every step — will be equipped for this moment.

Why last: Commercial e-plane charging at MCS power levels requires the full energy foundation, certified aviation hardware, regulatory approval, and the operational maturity that only comes from having navigated all previous layers. Crucially, airports that have actively managed grid congestion throughout the pyramid will be able to serve multi-megawatt charging without triggering expensive grid upgrades. This is where early movers gain a lasting competitive advantage.
MCS (Megawatt Charging System) 1–3 MW+ per gate Gate-integrated charging Grid-to-gate power management CPC aviation certification Smart load balancing
